§ 01 The Constraint

The pen plotter draws one continuous line. No fills, no gradients, no raster. Every algorithm in this catalog produces the same primitive output: a path for a pen. But the constraint does not reduce the algorithms to sameness. It transforms each one differently, and the transformations are the catalog's real subject.

Flow fields look organic on screen, where anti-aliased curves blend into soft gradients of direction. On paper, they become spidery. The pen has a fixed nib width, so line weight is constant everywhere the pen touches. Without variable opacity, every curve carries equal emphasis. The hierarchy that screen rendering provides for free, the subtle fade of distant streamlines, disappears entirely. What remains is a web of equal-weight strokes that exposes the underlying vector field with uncomfortable clarity. You see the math.

Fractals look busy on screen, where every branch and sub-branch renders simultaneously. On paper, they become meditative. The pen traces each recursion level in sequence. A tree that appears in a flash on screen takes twenty minutes to draw, and the viewer watching the plotter can see the structure build. Time becomes visible in the drawing itself: the first branches carry slightly more ink, where the pen paused at the start. The deeper levels, drawn faster as the pen warmed up, are lighter. The physical drawing encodes its own construction timeline.

Reaction-diffusion patterns fill a screen with dense, organic texture. On paper, they become fragile. The algorithm produces boundaries between regions, and without fill, those boundaries are the only marks on the page. A pattern that looks solid and biological on screen becomes a lattice of thin, trembling lines on paper. No fill means no mass. The paper stays mostly white, and the drawing reads as a skeletal diagram of a process rather than the process itself.

§ 03 Three That Surprised Us

The anomalies

Most algorithms produce what you expect. The catalog is interesting because of the ones that did not.

01 The algorithm that drew silence

Reaction-diffusion, plotted at 0.25mm

The screen render was bold: thick dark boundaries winding through bright regions, the classic Turing pattern. On screen it looked like aerial photography of a coral reef. Dense, alive, filling every pixel.

The plotted version was almost invisible. At 0.25mm nib width, the boundary lines were so thin they barely deposited ink. The paper's tooth caught the nib inconsistently, leaving gaps in what should have been continuous strokes. The result looked like pencil marks, not ink. Like someone had sketched the pattern freehand and then erased most of it.

I held the print at arm's length and it disappeared. Up close, under a desk lamp, the lines resolved into something unexpectedly beautiful: a fragile map of where two chemical populations had fought to a standstill. The violence of the simulation had been reduced to a whisper. I ran the same algorithm at 0.5mm and the effect vanished. It was only at the threshold of visibility that the drawing became something more than a reproduction.

02 The algorithm that drew time

Lorenz attractor, velocity-mapped

Strange attractors on screen are static. You see the full trajectory at once, every orbit stacked on top of the last. The structure is visible, but the dynamics are invisible. Fast and slow segments of the trajectory look identical.

The pen changed that. I mapped the attractor's instantaneous velocity to pen speed, and the plotter obliged. Where the trajectory moved slowly (near the attractor's fixed points, where the path spirals inward), the pen lingered. More ink per unit length. The strokes thickened. Where the trajectory flung between lobes, the pen raced. Hairline transitions, barely touching the paper.

The physical drawing encoded something the screen version could not: the attractor's temporal dynamics, frozen into line weight. The slow spirals were dark and heavy. The fast transitions were ghostly arcs. Looking at the finished piece, you could read the speed without knowing the math. A friend who knew nothing about dynamical systems picked it up and said, "It's fast here, right?" pointing at the thin arcs. The drawing had taught her the dynamics through ink alone.

03 The one the plotter refused

Space-filling curve, 50,000 segments

The algorithm generated a Hilbert curve variant at depth 8. Fifty thousand path segments. On screen, it rendered in under a second: a dense, satisfying labyrinth that filled a square completely. The SVG file was 4.2 megabytes.

The plotter started at 10am. By noon it had drawn the top third. The nib, a 0.3mm Sakura Micron, was performing well. Consistent line weight, no skipping. By 1pm, the halfway mark. Then, at roughly the 62% mark, the nib gave out. Not dramatically; it just started depositing less ink. By 65% the lines were fading. By 70% they were scratches.

I let it finish. Four hours and twelve minutes total. The top of the drawing was pristine: dense, mechanical, perfect. The bottom was a ruin. The same algorithm, the same parameters, the same nib width, degrading in real time. The transition zone, where the lines thinned from ink to scratch, was about two centimeters tall. I framed it. The intended output was a complete Hilbert curve. The actual output was a portrait of a pen dying. It was more interesting than anything the algorithm had planned.
